Main Content: The Science of the Grind and Roast

The Anatomy of the Roast

Roasting is the chemical process that converts raw, green coffee beans into the aromatic, brown beans we use for brewing. It's a pyrolysis reaction, involving heat to break down sugars and starches.

Roast Levels:

  • Light Roast: Higher acidity, brighter flavors, retains origin characteristics. Preferred for single-origin coffees and complex flavor profiles.
  • Medium Roast: Balanced acidity and body, commonly used for drip coffee. Caramel notes emerge as more sugars are caramelized.
  • Dark Roast: Low acidity, heavy body, dominant chocolate and smoky notes. Origin characteristics are replaced by roast flavors. The surface often appears oily due to the release of oils.

Grinder Quality: Burr vs. Blade

The quality of your grind is arguably the single most important factor in a good cup of coffee, often surpassing the quality of the coffee bean itself.

Blade Grinder: These are not grinders but choppers. They violently 'hack' the beans, creating an inconsistent mix of very fine powder (fines) and large chunks. This results in uneven extraction, leading to a simultaneously sour and bitter cup.

Burr Grinder: These crush the beans between two abrasive surfaces (burrs), resulting in a uniform particle size. This consistency is vital for predictable and balanced extraction.

Coffee Fact

The Maillard reaction, a key process during roasting, is the same chemical reaction responsible for browning bread and searing meat. It creates hundreds of different flavor compounds in the coffee bean.
