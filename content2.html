Main Content: Water and Brewing Methods

The Chemistry of Water

Water constitutes over 98% of a cup of coffee, making it the most critical, yet often overlooked, ingredient. It is a solvent, and its mineral content dictates how effectively it extracts flavor compounds.

The ideal brewing water has a Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) range of 100-250 ppm. Specifically, a balance of calcium and magnesium hardness is crucial for pulling desirable flavors from the grounds.

Brewing Method Deep Dive

Immersion (French Press)

This method involves full saturation of the coffee grounds for a set period. It is simple, delivers a heavy body, and is forgiving with measurement. However, it requires a coarse grind to prevent excessive fines from passing through the mesh filter.

Parameters: Coarse grind, 4-minute steep time, typically 1:15 ratio.

Pour Over (V60, Chemex)

This is a percolation method where water is slowly poured over grounds, relying on gravity. It demands precision (grind size, water temperature, pour speed) but yields a clean, bright, and nuanced cup due to the paper filter removing oils and fines.

Parameters: Medium-fine grind, 2:30-3:30 total brew time, 1:16 ratio.

Brewing Temperature

The ideal brewing temperature range is 90°C to 96°C (195°F to 205°F). Water that is too cold leads to sour, under-extracted coffee. Boiling water (100°C) can scald the grounds and extract undesirable bitter compounds too quickly.
