                    <h3>The Chemistry of Water</h3>

                    <p>Water constitutes over 98% of a cup of coffee, making it the most critical, yet often overlooked, ingredient. It is a solvent, and its mineral content dictates how effectively it extracts flavor compounds.</p>

                    <p>The ideal brewing water has a Total Dissolved Solids (TDS) range of 100-250 ppm. Specifically, a balance of calcium and magnesium hardness is crucial for pulling desirable flavors from the grounds.</p>

                </section>


                <section>

                    <h3>Brewing Method Deep Dive</h3>

                    <div class="content-with-image">

                        <img src="https://placehold.co/600x400/00796b/ffffff?text=French+Press" alt="A French press device on a wooden table.">

                        <h4>Immersion (French Press)</h4>

                        <p>This method involves full saturation of the coffee grounds for a set period. It is simple, delivers a heavy body, and is forgiving with measurement. However, it requires a coarse grind to prevent excessive fines from passing through the mesh filter.</p>

                        <p><strong>Parameters:</strong> Coarse grind, 4-minute steep time, typically 1:15 ratio.</p>

                    </div>


                    <div class="content-with-image" style="clear:both;">

                        <img src="https://placehold.co/600x400/0288d1/ffffff?text=V60+Pour" alt="A V60 pour over device with a kettle pouring water.">

                        <h4>Pour Over (V60, Chemex)</h4>

                        <p>This is a percolation method where water is slowly poured over grounds, relying on gravity. It demands precision (grind size, water temperature, pour speed) but yields a clean, bright, and nuanced cup due to the paper filter removing oils and fines.</p>

                        <p><strong>Parameters:</strong> Medium-fine grind, 2:30-3:30 total brew time, 1:16 ratio.</p>

                    </div>

                <h3>Brewing Temperature</h3>

                <p>The ideal brewing temperature range is 90°C to 96°C (195°F to 205°F). Water that is too cold leads to sour, under-extracted coffee. Boiling water (100°C) can scald the grounds and extract undesirable bitter compounds too quickly.</p>
